# The research behind calm me down

Every technique in the app was chosen because it is **fast-acting**, **body-first**,
and **supported by published research** — the qualities that matter when someone
is flooded, going blank, and past the point where reasoning or talking helps.

A recurring design principle comes straight from the evidence: **calm the body
before the mind.** When a person is in an emotional flood ("amygdala hijack"),
the thinking brain is effectively offline, which is why *nothing you say lands*.
The session therefore leads with physiological tools (breath, temperature,
grounding) and only later moves to reflective ones (naming feelings,
self-compassion).

---

## 1. Cyclic sighing (the physiological sigh)

Two inhales (a big one, then a small top-up) followed by a long, slow exhale.

A 2023 randomized controlled study from Stanford (Balban, Yilmaz; Spiegel &
Huberman labs), published in *Cell Reports Medicine*, had 111 people do five
minutes a day of one of three breathwork styles or mindfulness meditation for a
month. **Cyclic sighing produced the greatest improvement in mood and the
biggest reduction in breathing rate — more than mindfulness meditation.** The
extended exhale raises intrathoracic pressure and increases venous return,
triggering parasympathetic ("calming brake") slowing of the heart via
respiratory sinus arrhythmia.

- Stanford Medicine: <https://med.stanford.edu/news/insights/2023/02/cyclic-sighing-can-help-breathe-away-anxiety.html>
- Study (PubMed): <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36630953/>

## 2. DBT TIPP — temperature, intense exercise, paced breathing, paired muscle relaxation

TIPP is the distress-tolerance skill set from Dialectical Behavior Therapy for
**intense, crisis-level emotion**. All four change body chemistry within seconds
to minutes by shifting the autonomic nervous system from fight-or-flight toward
calm. The app uses three of them:

- **Temperature — "Cool the wave."** Cold water on the face activates the
  **mammalian dive reflex**, which rapidly slows heart rate and pulls the body
  out of overdrive. It works even when words don’t, which is exactly what’s
  needed when someone has "gone blank."
- **Paced breathing — "Box breathing."** Slow, equal-length breaths keep the
  system from tipping into arousal.
- **Paired muscle relaxation — "Tense and release."** Tensing then releasing
  muscle groups physically lowers arousal (see also #4).

- Overview: <https://www.mindfulteen.org/dbt/distress-tolerance/tipp-skills/>
- Dive reflex / temperature: <https://www.sevenriverscounselling.com/sevenriversblog/dbt-skills-for-emotional-regulation-the-cold-water-tipp-technique-explained>

## 3. 5-4-3-2-1 sensory grounding

Naming five things you can see, four you can feel, three you can hear, two you
can smell, and one slow breath. When the mind floods or dissociates ("goes
blank/foggy"), orienting to concrete present-moment sensory detail interrupts
the spiral and re-engages the thinking brain. It is a core, widely used
trauma-informed grounding technique for acute anxiety and dissociation.

## 4. Progressive muscle relaxation (PMR)

Systematically tensing and releasing muscle groups. Developed by Edmund Jacobson,
PMR is a long-established, evidence-based method for lowering physiological
arousal and anxiety; it also appears as the "paired muscle relaxation" P in DBT’s
TIPP.

## 5. Affect labelling — "name it to tame it"

Putting a feeling into words. A landmark UCLA fMRI study (Lieberman et al., 2007,
*Psychological Science*, "Putting Feelings Into Words") showed that **labelling
an emotion reduces amygdala activity** — the brain’s alarm — while engaging the
right ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (the regulating, thinking region). The
effect has replicated across many studies. You don’t have to fix or resolve the
feeling; naming it is enough to turn the volume down. Crucially, the app does
*not* ask the person to problem-solve in this state — only to name.

- UCLA Health: <https://www.uclahealth.org/news/release/putting-feelings-into-words-produces-therapeutic-effects-in-the-brain-ucla-neuroimaging-study-supports-ancient-buddhist-teachings>
- Study (PubMed): <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17576282/>

## 6. Self-compassion break

Acknowledging the hurt ("this is a hard moment"), remembering common humanity
("hard moments are part of being human — I’m not alone"), and offering oneself
kindness. Drawn from Kristin Neff’s self-compassion research, this reliably
lowers distress and self-criticism and is especially useful when someone is
being harsh with themselves.

## 7. Safe-place imagery & orienting to safety

Vividly recalling a personal safe place cues the body toward safety. This draws
on Stephen Porges’ **polyvagal theory**: cues of safety help shift the nervous
system out of defensive states. The app captures a personal "safe anchor" during
onboarding and gently reflects it back.

## For young children (age ≤ 9)

The same principles are translated into concrete, playful, developmentally
appropriate forms — the standard toolkit of pediatric and school-counseling
practice:

- **Buddy belly breaths** — a stuffed animal on the belly makes slow
  diaphragmatic breathing visible and fun.
- **"Smell the flower, blow out the candle"** — the simplest way to teach a young
  child a long, calming exhale.
- **Star breathing** — tracing a shape while breathing pairs a calming exhale
  with a simple motor task a preschooler can follow.
- **Naming the feeling with faces** — an early, picture-based version of affect
  labelling.

## How the app measures whether it worked

The same five-question check-in plus the 1–10 headline is taken before and after
the session. Scores are blended into a single 0–10 "state" number (the headline
weighted a little more heavily, since the user identified it as the primary
signal). A session is counted as a **win when that state improves by more than
10%**. Because a tiny starting number would make any change look huge, the
percentage is computed against a floored baseline so wins stay meaningful.

## How it adapts

After each session the app nudges the effectiveness weight of every technique
used, based on how much the state score actually improved and an optional
thumbs-up / thumbs-down. Over time the highest-scoring techniques for *this*
person surface more often, a small amount of rotation keeps sessions from going
stale, and severe check-ins are front-loaded with the fastest body-based tools
(cyclic sighing, cold reset, grounding).
